Sonatype Guide

Designing a developer-facing security intelligence platform that shifts open-source security from reactive enforcement to proactive, confident component selection.

Enterprise SaaS Developer-Facing Platform Security Intelligence

Executive Summary

Context

Guide centralized open-source component research and surfaced security intelligence at the moment of selection, combining public ecosystem data with Sonatype's proprietary risk insights inside a unified web interface and MCP server.

Role

As Senior Product Designer, I led end-to-end product design across problem framing, interaction architecture, and scalable system patterns in close partnership with product and engineering leadership.

Challenge

Developers were forced to interpret vulnerability severity, policy enforcement logic, and component health signals only after enforcement events, often across disconnected systems.

Objective

Make high-stakes security decisions easier by clarifying rule hierarchy, surfacing remediation paths, and improving state visibility across complex workflows.

Outcome

Delivered a unified decision surface that reduced reactive rework, clarified policy intent, and established a scalable foundation for proactive open-source security decisions.

Problem Scope

Enterprise organizations are shifting security left, placing greater responsibility for open-source risk decisions on development teams. From component selection to risk remediation, developers must make decisions in a complex environment where context is fragmented across systems.

Component research happens in a browser. Enforcement happens in CI. Remediation details live in vulnerability reports. Each system provides part of the picture, but none present the full decision context.

As a result, developers are forced to:

  • Re-evaluate components reactively after enforcement
  • Restart research cycles under time pressure
  • Switch between browser, IDE, CI logs, and vulnerability dashboards
  • Interpret policy logic without visibility into why a component is blocked

Guide needed to surface security intelligence by default, unifying policy logic, vulnerability severity, dependency context, and remediation guidance within a single decision surface.

Project Constraints

Rapid GTM timeline

Product definition, monetization strategy, marketing positioning, and engineering execution progressed in parallel, requiring scope discipline and decisions that could support both launch and expansion.

Code-first design mandate

Leadership required the product to be designed directly in code rather than through static mockups. The design process itself had to support live prototyping and production-aligned behavior (see Guide UX Lab).

Design system requirements

Guide was built using an established third-party design system rather than Sonatype's internal system. Customizations had to remain minimal, requiring thoughtful adaptation rather than reinvention.

Theming and responsive layouts

Data-heavy interfaces had to preserve hierarchy, contrast, and legibility across themes and breakpoints. UIs were required to support both light and dark modes and adapt fluidly across viewport sizes.

Free-to-paid architecture

Monetization infrastructure was a launch requirement. The experience needed to support gated proprietary intelligence, tier differentiation, usage limits, billing workflows, and visible credit tracking from the initial release.

Generalized policy logic at launch

Guide is not a policy engine and does not support custom policy configuration. The interface needed to communicate generalized risk and policy concepts without misleading users about configurability.

Individual to Enterprise scale

The product had to serve students and individual developers as effectively as enterprise security teams. The interface needed to scale across levels of expertise and organizational complexity.

Dual audience alignment

Experiences needed to feel intuitive and accessible for net-new developers and existing Sonatype customers. Patterns and terminology had to bridge familiarity and approachability.

Design Strategy

Guide required more than visual simplification. The goal was to shift open-source security from reactive enforcement to proactive decision-making, within a compressed release timeline and a monetized product model. Across each decision, the goal was not to eliminate complexity, but to organize and clarify it.

01

Surface intelligence by default

Security context was surfaced at the browsing stage, making risk signals visible before enforcement events or selection decisions. Developers did not need to trigger enforcement or initiate analysis to understand risk.

02

Make policy logic visible

Rather than abstracting enforcement outcomes, the experience prioritized transparency around why a component is risky, how severity is evaluated, and what remediation paths are available. Rule hierarchy and state relationships were treated as first-class UI elements.

03

Design for scalable interpretation

Guide had to support individual developers and enterprise teams alike. Information architecture emphasized progressive disclosure, allowing rapid triage while preserving depth for deliberate analysis.

04

Build durable interaction patterns

Given the accelerated timeline and the need for design-in-code, the solution relied on reusable, system-aligned interaction patterns. Decisions were made at the model level first, ensuring the interface reflected real data relationships rather than visual groupings.

Workflow Architecture

Guide was designed as a relational security intelligence system, not simply a component lookup tool. Components, vulnerabilities, and documentation could be searched independently, but their records were interlinked, allowing developers to move between artifacts without losing context.

The architecture was anchored by global search as the primary entry point into the system. From the moment a user arrived, search unified discovery, validation, and navigation across artifact types.

Three structural shifts defined the experience:

1. Informed Browsing

Traditional workflows surface security implications only after a component has been adopted, often during CI enforcement or policy checks. Guide reframed this by embedding Trust Score, policy compliance status, and vulnerability signals directly into web browsing and code editors.

Intelligence was exposed during exploration rather than deferred to enforcement. Developers could assess component health while browsing, before integration decisions were made.

2. Explicit Risk Relationships

Open-source security decisions involve layered relationships between components, vulnerabilities, dependency chains, and policy interpretation. Guide made those relationships visible rather than implicit.

Each record surfaced Trust score indicators, policy compliance status, vulnerability severity and exploitability, and dependencies and dependents through structured tabs. Components and vulnerabilities could be explored independently, but their interdependencies were always navigable.

3. Search as the Primary Interface

Guide treated search as infrastructure rather than utility. For both logged-out and logged-in users, the experience centered on a persistent global search field that unified components, vulnerabilities, and documentation.

Search supported predictive matches, direct routing for exact IDs, categorized result views with tab-level counts, and contextual filtering within each result type. The system balanced precision and discovery without introducing separate modes.

Key Interaction Patterns

Guide relied on a set of structural interaction patterns designed to scale across search, record views, and monetization states.

Trust Score as a Composite Signal

Trust Score was developed as a first-class metric to provide immediate context at the version level. It synthesized vulnerability severity and prevalence, availability of safe versions, package popularity and maintenance, release cadence, and known malware detection.

The score was version-specific, reinforcing that risk posture can vary between releases of the same component. Each result card surfaced a numerical Trust Score (0-100), paired with a color signal and iconography to communicate sentiment at a glance.

Binary Policy Compliance with Transparent Criteria

Policy compliance was intentionally simplified at the browsing level to a binary pass or fail state, represented by a green check or red X.

On detail pages, compliance expanded into explicit criteria including malware detection, CVSS threshold, and copyleft licensing. This preserved simplicity at the surface while maintaining transparency in the underlying logic.

Pivot-Based Record Architecture

Each record page, whether a component or a vulnerability, functioned as a relational hub rather than a static detail view.

The tab model allowed users to pivot laterally across related records without restarting their search or losing context, encouraging movement through relationships instead of isolated pages.

Global Search as Intent-Aware Navigation

Search was persistent in the top navigation and served as the primary orientation anchor across the interface.

It supported predictive dropdown results, exact-match routing for component coordinates or vulnerability IDs, categorized result pages with counts, and contextual filtering within each artifact type.

Progressive Disclosure Through Gated Intelligence

Guide supported logged out, registered (free), and paid user states while keeping public information discoverable.

Proprietary intelligence including Trust Score, policy indicators, recommended versions, and deeper vulnerability insights was gated with contextual blur, tooltips, and sign-up prompts.

Credit Visibility and Tier Signaling

Free users received a limited monthly credit allotment with persistent usage indicators and proactive notifications as limits approached, while paid tiers received higher allowances.

All users had access to a Usage page displaying current status and historical trends, positioning credit usage as visible infrastructure rather than hidden constraint.

Collaboration

Guide was developed during a period of significant organizational transition, with broad role eliminations scheduled to take effect on the product's fixed December release date. One week after receiving notice, I was selected to lead design for a new executive initiative, working in close partnership with leadership to define and ship a net-new product under that same deadline.

With no existing product foundation and a fixed launch date, alignment across leadership, product, and engineering became the primary operating mode. I worked closely with the Director of Design and the Chief Product Development Officer to clarify scope boundaries, navigate tradeoffs, and continuously reconcile requirements against constraints. The UX Lab became central to these conversations, functioning as a shared environment where workflows could be stress-tested against real data rather than debated abstractly.

Engineering partnership shaped the product at every stage. As interaction patterns and information models took form inside the UX Lab, engineering leadership and subject matter experts reviewed the experience directly, surfacing feasibility concerns and implementation implications early. This approach allowed implementation sequencing to reflect actual user journeys rather than documentation handoffs, helping maintain momentum despite the compressed timeline.

Product Marketing collaboration unfolded in parallel. Naming, visual direction, and launch positioning needed to progress before the product formally existed. Because corporate and product branding systems differed, deliberate coordination ensured Guide felt distinct yet aligned. The UX Lab provided Marketing with a functional, data-rich environment they could reference while developing the landing page and messaging strategy.

Rather than initiating new discovery workshops, we synthesized insights from Sonatype's existing research repository, including prior user interviews, design sprints, and workshop outputs. Once the UX Lab reached a stable state, we conducted targeted usability sessions to validate search behavior, signal clarity, and monetization touchpoints before release.

Progress and alignment were maintained through weekly team check-ins, bi-weekly engineering demos, monthly product organization updates, and executive reviews as needed. Visibility remained high throughout development, and the release date remained unchanged.

Guide was shaped through continuous cross-functional iteration under fixed constraints and sustained executive visibility.

Outcomes

On-time launch

Guide launched on its scheduled release date as a fully functional security intelligence platform, establishing a new entry point into Sonatype's ecosystem.

Strong internal alignment

Internal reception was strong across product, engineering, and executive leadership, with Trust Score and policy transparency resonating as a clear, differentiated evaluation model.

Early customer commitment

External response reinforced demand. Existing enterprise customers responded positively during early demos, and several major organizations committed to subscriptions prior to general availability.

Code-first delivery gains

Engineering adoption of the UX Lab changed implementation dynamics by grounding workflow sequencing and feasibility discussions in production-aligned behavior rather than static documentation.

Validated product model

Guide shipped not only as a new product, but as a validated model for proactive open-source intelligence and code-first product design.

Reflection

Designing Guide reinforced that complexity is not the enemy of usability. Fragmentation is.

When relationships between components, vulnerabilities, and policy logic are explicit, developers can navigate sophisticated systems without friction. Architectural clarity enables confidence.

The project also underscored the practical advantages of designing within environments that mirror real constraints. Modeling workflows in code exposed structural assumptions early and aligned cross-functional stakeholders around a shared, inspectable artifact.

Finally, Guide demonstrated the importance of building products that scale across user sophistication. Individual developers and enterprise teams often require the same intelligence, but at different levels of depth. Flexible architecture, rather than isolated screens, makes that scaling possible.
