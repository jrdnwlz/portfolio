Sonatype Guide

A product design case study focused on clearer security decisions in configuration-heavy, developer-facing workflows.

Role: Senior Product Designer Timeframe: 2024 to 2025 Tools: Figma, Workshop Facilitation, Enterprise Design Systems

Featured Work Summary

Designing to: help developers and security teams make secure open-source decisions with better state visibility and clearer policy outcomes.

Developer-Facing Platform Strategy Rule Hierarchy Decision Confidence

Executive Summary

Context

Guide introduced a new way to evaluate open-source packages, vulnerabilities, and trust signals inside an enterprise SaaS environment.

Role

I led product design across problem framing, interaction architecture, and system-level pattern decisions with product and engineering partners.

Challenge

Users had to combine policy logic, vulnerability severity, and package quality signals under time pressure. Important context was often split across views.

Objective

Improve clarity for high-stakes decisions by making rule hierarchy, remediation path, and waiver impact easier to interpret.

Outcome

The updated structure improved clarity during review sessions and gave teams a stronger baseline for consistent workflow behavior.

Problem / Context

Developers and security engineers were making frequent package decisions across dense datasets, policy checks, and dependency graphs. Existing flows made it hard to answer three key questions quickly: what is blocked, why it is blocked, and what path is available now.

The product needed to support fast triage and deliberate analysis in the same interface, without obscuring policy intent or remediation tradeoffs.

Constraints

  • Configuration-heavy workflows had to remain flexible for different enterprise policy models.
  • Security language and status states needed to stay precise across product surfaces.
  • Patterns had to align with existing platform behavior so teams could adopt them without heavy rework.

Approach / Strategy

I structured the work around decision points instead of screens. The design strategy focused on hierarchy first: signal relevance, policy precedence, and traceable action paths. This reduced context switching and improved continuity between triage, analysis, and execution.

Design reviews used scenario-based walkthroughs to validate confidence under realistic conditions, including edge-case dependencies and competing policy outcomes.

Key Patterns / Decisions

  • Unified state panel for risk, policy, and remediation to reduce cross-page interpretation.
  • Consistent status language for blocked, waivable, and approved paths.
  • Progressive disclosure for dense technical metadata so users can scan first and inspect deeper on demand.

Collaboration

This work required close alignment between product, security domain experts, and engineering. We used working sessions to map rule behavior and review edge cases, then validated interaction details in iterative design reviews tied to release planning milestones.

Outcomes

  • Improved clarity in policy interpretation across key decision surfaces.
  • Stronger alignment on expected behavior for rule overrides and waivers.
  • A reusable foundation for future platform workflows that depend on decision confidence.

Reflection / Learnings

In security contexts, visual polish matters less than decision traceability. The most valuable shift was treating rule hierarchy as core product architecture, not just policy copy. Future iterations should continue to strengthen state explanations for mixed-expertise teams.
